LoggerPro®

This is the vernier.com software used in our computer/sensor-based experiments at SCCC. Many experiments in the physics laboratory at SCCC use computer-based equipment.

It is also a software that can be used for video analysis, or simple graphical analysis of any data.

LoggerPro is licensed to SCCC faculty and students. SCCC students can download LoggerPro and install in their personal computer, for free. Video Analysis

Video analysis is fun, powerful, and easy to do.

Anyone can record a movie with a phone, for example, upload it to a computer, and easily analyse the motion with a software like LoggerPro or Tracker. You can get measurements of speed, acceleration, force, momentum, energy, period, frequency, damping constant, etc! You can fully examine, characterize and understand the motion.

Click on the links below for video tutorials on how to use LoggerPro to do video analysis:

  • Using movies already contained in Logger Pro to learn video analysis.
  • Inserting your own video file and analysing it.

In one of the tutorials, you've seen how tracking the motion of a basketball allows you to get a pretty good value for the acceleration of gravity on Earth (-9.8 m/s2). Here's an exercise/challenge for you to try:

  • This is a 1972 video (*.mov) of an Apollo 16 astronaut jumping on the Moon. You can download it, insert it in LoggerPro, track the motion of the astronaut while jumping (it's best to track the position of the top-left corner of the backpack against the black background, frame-by-frame), and get the acceleration of gravity on the Moon (-1.6 m/s2), similarly to what was done for the basketball. You will need to calibrate the scale of the movie using astronaut+backpack height as 2 meters: calibration is essential in video analysis. This video is from the project LivePhoto Physics.

The LivePhoto Physics website is a good initial source of videos for analysis, until you start recording your own movies.

To record your own movies for video analysis, be mindful to: 1) add a scaling reference in the frame of the video, like a ruler placed on the same plane (same distance from camera) where the motion occurs, or record the size of a landmark aligned with the plane of motion – scaling is likely to be your main source of error; 2) avoid recording something too close to the camera because of lens distortion effects; 3) avoid moving the camera to follow a motion: it makes scaling very hard if your scaling reference shifts out of frame; 4) you will need a lot more light than you think, in order to get decent images when using a high frame rate (direct sunlight is a good idea); 5) make sure you know what frame rate you are using (30 fps for most cellphones): you will need to insert this information in the software.
